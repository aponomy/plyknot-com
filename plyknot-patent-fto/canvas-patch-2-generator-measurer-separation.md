## §4.x Generator-Measurer Separation (GMS)

When language-model measurers participate in pipelines that both generate content (recorded as `predict()`) and evaluate content (recorded as `measure()`), their substrate `depends` declarations must be disjoint between the generator role and the measurer role. This is a hard architectural constraint, not a style preference.

The constraint addresses a contamination mode that does not appear in the framework's original physics-grounded operating set. A Penning trap cannot generate a measurement value of its own electron-mass result; the apparatus produces what the apparatus produces, and a separate apparatus produces a separate result. Language-model pipelines do not have this property. An LLM can generate text and then measure the same text, and when those operations share substrate the measurement is not independent of the generation. The model optimises generation toward measurement features it would itself surface, and the resulting measurement is contaminated with the generator's own preferences.

The constraint is structurally simple. Every coupling entry already carries a `measurer` identifier (the entity that produced the value) and that identifier resolves to a substrate `depends` declaration. The framework computes `substrate_overlap(generator, measurer)` for every (generator, measurer) pair in any pipeline that combines `predict()` and `measure()` operations, and rejects measurement records produced by measurers whose substrate-dependency declaration overlaps non-trivially (default threshold: > 0.0 overlap, i.e., any shared substrate node) with the generator's substrate-dependency declaration for the formulation under evaluation.

The patent-FTO repulsive-broadening loop is the first concrete instantiation. When Opus proposes a broader claim formulation, the substrate-overlap check forbids any Anthropic-substrate measurer from evaluating the result. Gemini-Pro and a Google-substrate embedding model carry the evaluation. The proposer's substrate never measures its own output. The same constraint applies to the dual-model rendering pipeline (any case where one LLM drafts text and another LLM scores or critiques it), to active-learning pipelines that propose experiments and then measure outcomes, and to any future agent-system architecture that combines hypothesis generation and hypothesis evaluation in a closed loop.

GMS interacts with two other architectural rules in informative ways. First, the LLM-as-Judge elimination from §9.4 forbids using LLMs to render verdicts on coupling-map entries; GMS extends the principle by forbidding same-substrate generation-and-evaluation even when the evaluation is structural rather than judgmental. Second, RAG-grounded methodology adaptation (the practice of injecting domain-specific reference material into a measurer's prompt context) does not change a measurer's substrate `depends` because retrieval-grounding is data-via-context-window, not a different reasoning engine. Two `opus-with-different-RAG-indices` measurements share full substrate and the GMS check correctly treats them as same-measurer. RAG identifiers belong in the `method` field of a coupling entry, not in the substrate declaration.

The framework's existing echo-chamber detection is the same machinery operating in a different mode. Echo-chamber detection identifies post-hoc that two measurers agreed because they share substrate; GMS forbids the same shared-substrate condition pre-hoc whenever generation and measurement are paired. The two checks are dual.

A practical note: GMS limits the measurer panel available for generation-paired pipelines to those vendors not used in generation. The framework's response is to maintain a deliberately diverse vendor roster precisely so that GMS leaves a non-empty measurer set for any plausible generation choice.
